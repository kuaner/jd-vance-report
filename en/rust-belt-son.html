Chapter I

Son of the Rust Belt Son of the Rust Belt

A Middletown childhood, the Marine Corps, and Yale — how trauma forged the core convictions of a political figure

Late autumn, 2005. Al-Asad Airbase in Iraq's western Anbar Province. A 21-year-old U.S. Marine corporal named James David Hamel stood outside a polling station for Iraq's constitutional referendum, a rifle slung over one shoulder and a notebook tucked under the other. He was reading Natan Sharansky's The Case for Democracy — a book treated as gospel by the George W. Bush administration. But the Iraqis before him did not yearn for democracy as the book promised — they only wanted a safe country. Months later, Vice President Dick Cheney flew into the base on an inspection visit. A young soldier dared to ask whether things were truly "looking good." Cheney smiled and said, "Well, Iraq's looking good." That night, Hamel wrote in his journal his first deep skepticism toward the American government.

Seven years later, he had renamed himself J.D. Vance, graduated from Yale Law School, become a protege of Peter Thiel, and written a memoir that would define a generation's political imagination. Eight years after that, he stood at the swearing-in ceremony in Washington, D.C., as the 50th Vice President of the United States.

The core of this story is not "a poor boy's rise from rags to riches." It is about a person who witnessed, amid the chaos of his childhood, how "learned helplessness" could devour a family, a community — and then found the antidote in Marine Corps discipline, only to re-examine the limits of that antidote in Yale's elite world. His fixation on "hard work versus laziness," his diagnosis of "cultural decline," his distrust of the elite class — all of it traces back to three places: Middletown, Ohio; Cherry Point, North Carolina; and New Haven, Connecticut.

Section I

Three Names

The name on J.D. Vance's first birth certificate was James Donald Bowman, born August 2, 1984, in Middletown, Butler County, Ohio. The "Donald" came from his biological father, Donald Bowman — a man who left the home while J.D. was still a toddler. This surname vanished when he was six. His mother, Beverly, married her third husband, Bob Hamel, who adopted the young boy. Beverly changed his middle name from "Donald" to "David" (her uncle's name) to preserve the "J.D." nickname while erasing the trace of his biological father. Vance later wrote: "This seemed a bit of a stretch even when I was six. Any old D name would have done, so long as it wasn't Donald." [Note 1]

His third name change came in 2013, by which time he had already graduated from Yale Law School. After his mother and adoptive father Bob Hamel divorced, his maternal grandparents became his only real anchor. He changed his surname to his grandmother Bonnie Vance's maiden name — Vance. He told The New York Times it was to honor the woman who had pulled him back from the darkest moments of his life. During his 2022 Senate campaign, he formally abbreviated "James David" to "J.D."

Three name changes. Three families. Three identities. A story about "absence" was etched into those names: a biological father who vanished, a mother whose husbands came and went, and grandparents who became the ultimate safe harbor.

On social media, this pattern of continual identity reinvention drew considerable scrutiny. Twitter user @joni_askola wrote in February 2026: "JD Vance changed his name, his religion, and his entire political ideology to become the exact avatar his billionaire backer Peter Thiel required. When a politician reinvents their identity to serve an oligarch, they are an asset." The tweet received over 31,000 likes and more than 9,000 retweets [Note 2]. White supremacist Nick Fuentes attacked from the opposite extreme: "So where does JD Vance actually come from? ... created in a petri dish by David Frum and Peter Thiel." [Note 3]

These attacks — whether from the left or the far right — all pointed to the same vulnerability: the plasticity of Vance's identity. But that plasticity itself was precisely the product of a Rust Belt upbringing. When a child grows up constantly changing fathers, constantly moving, constantly adapting to new family structures, "identity" is never solid from the start — it is liquid.

Section II

Middletown — A Forgotten City

Middletown sits between Dayton and Cincinnati, with a population of roughly 51,000. The city's fate was once deeply bound to AK Steel (formerly Armco) — the postwar destination for Appalachian migrants heading north, and the reason Vance's maternal grandparents, Bonnie and James Vance, moved from Jackson, Kentucky after World War II. They were part of the "Great Appalachian Migration": Scots-Irish mountain families leaving their impoverished homeland for factory jobs in industrial cities.

But by the time of Vance's childhood in the 1990s, that factory was no longer an anchor of hope. Vance described Middletown as "a mostly low-income town where, for many families with Appalachian roots, 'poverty was a family tradition'" [Note 4]

The scene inside the home was more suffocating than the decay of the neighborhood outside.

His mother, Beverly, was a nurse — a profession that gave her easy access to prescription drugs, and thus a fifteen-year abyss of addiction. She married five times. Vance deployed a precise metaphor for his own childhood: "Of all the things I hated about my childhood, nothing compared to the revolving door of father figures." [Note 5]

When he was twelve, Beverly was arrested during a driving incident. She accelerated, threatening to crash the car and kill them both. Vance jumped out and ran to a neighbor's house to call for help. Years later, he told Megyn Kelly: "In that moment I just felt relieved and I thought to myself, 'Alright I'm going to live another day.'" [Note 6]

Later, during Vance's time at Yale Law School, his mother collapsed from a heroin overdose. A mother who worked as a nurse — with prescription drugs within arm's reach and a progressively worsening heroin addiction — ultimately reduced to a body in a hospital bed. Vance wrote in Hillbilly Elegy: "Addictions based on grim future without hope." [Note 7]

Section III

Mamaw — Iron Will

Bonnie Blanton Vance — an Appalachian woman with no higher education but a will of iron. She and her husband, James Vance (Papaw), came to Ohio from Breathitt County, Kentucky after World War II. Vance called them "without question or qualification, the best things that ever happened to me" [Note 8].

Mamaw believed in Jesus and loved Billy Graham's sermons, but detested "organized religion." When Vance, around ten years old, asked her "Does God love us?", she wept [Note 9]. This scene would recur throughout Vance's later religious transformation — a child searching for meaning amid chaos, and a grandmother who could offer no theological answers but whose love was never absent.

"Someone will need to care for the family when she is gone, and that he has the choice to make something of himself."

When Beverly's behavior became intolerable, Vance was placed in his grandparents' household. Mamaw was stern but fiercely loving — once, when Vance and a group of friends damaged her car while fooling around, she checked herself out of the hospital where she was being treated for pneumonia, just to bring him home. She told him: "Someone will need to care for the family when she is gone, and that he has the choice to make something of himself." [Note 10]

Vance later said in his 2022 Senate victory speech: "You're not always going to agree with every vote that I take...but I will never forget the woman who raised me." [Note 11]

Mamaw's presence was crucial, not only because she provided a stable home environment, but because she embodied the prototype of the "hard work versus laziness" binary that became central to Vance's worldview. Mamaw was the woman who came out of the Kentucky mountains and gritted her teeth to hold a family together in a working-class community beside a steel mill. She did not speak of "systemic injustice" or "structural poverty" — she spoke only of "whether you had a choice." This mode of thinking would later become the core framework of Vance's diagnosis of Rust Belt poverty.

In 2024, at the Republican National Convention, Vance publicly celebrated his mother Beverly's long-awaited achievement of sustained recovery from addiction. After rehab, she gave a public speech framed as "a story of addiction, hope, and redemption." In September 2025, as Vice President, Vance hosted an event at the White House Roosevelt Room to celebrate her recovery milestones [Note 12]. This ending seemed heartwarming, but it concealed a deeper issue: Vance attributed his mother's eventual success to personal willpower rather than any institutional support. This pattern of attribution — individual choice over structural forces — would run through his entire political career.

Section IV

"College, or Nothing, or the Marine Corps"

In April 2003, one month after the Iraq War began, 18-year-old James David Hamel walked into the Middletown recruiting office. Accompanying him was his older cousin, Rachael — a former Marine. He had originally planned to attend Ohio State University, but mediocre high school grades and chaotic financial aid paperwork dissuaded him.

"There was college, or there was nothing, or there was the Marine Corps...and I didn't like either of the first two options."

Most of his family fiercely opposed his enlistment. But the decision — driven less by patriotism than by a desire to escape — uprooted him from the chaos of Middletown and thrust him into the character-forging crucible of Parris Island.

He scored high enough on physical fitness tests to be assigned to the Marine Corps' Public Affairs specialty. In July 2004, he was posted to MCAS Cherry Point in eastern North Carolina, attached to the 2nd Marine Aircraft Wing, holding the rank of Corporal (E-4) [Note 14].

During those days at Cherry Point, Vance's fellow Marines remembered a distinctly incongruous figure:

  • He devoured The Economist and launched into extended policy debates with his barracks mates
  • He was "more aggressively enthusiastic about John McCain than a normal person would be"
  • He read Ayn Rand novels in the barracks and urged others to do the same
  • He had a gift for solving Rubik's cubes at remarkable speed
  • One fellow Marine superimposed his face over a photo of George W. Bush and said: "You can just look at his face and know — this guy always has a plan" [Note 15]

A kid from the bottom of the Rust Belt, reading The Economist and Ayn Rand in a Marine Corps barracks, thinking about free markets and individualism. This was inherently a contradiction — a person cast by fate into the underclass, yet absorbing an ideology that blamed poverty on the individual.

In August 2005, Vance was deployed to Al-Asad Airbase in western Iraq, serving as a combat correspondent for six months [Note 16].

It was a nuanced position. He did not see the front lines — his mission was to follow the troops and write brief dispatches for military publications and local newspapers. The base itself was considered safe by Iraqi standards — jokingly nicknamed "Camp Cupcake" because it had a Burger King and a Pizza Hut. But mortar attacks were commonplace.

The most critical experience came in late 2005. Iraq was holding its constitutional referendum and parliamentary elections, and Vance was assigned to provide security at polling stations. He held Sharansky's The Case for Democracy in his hands, while the Iraqis before him "didn't care that much about democracy...they just wanted a safe country." This gap — the chasm between elite grand narratives and the basic needs of ordinary people — would later become the bedrock of Vance's political philosophy.

But there is a deeper point worth noting: Vance did not see actual combat in Iraq. He acknowledged this himself [Note 18]. What does this mean? A politician who would later express skepticism about "forever wars" — his anti-war stance came not from PTSD or bloody battlefield memories, but from a cooler kind of observation. He saw the rupture between Washington's narrative and Baghdad's reality. Cheney's "Iraq's looking good" was not merely a political slogan to a young soldier experiencing the chaos firsthand — it was a betrayal.

Section V

"Learned Helplessness" — From Psychological Concept to Political Weapon

In Hillbilly Elegy, Vance introduced a critical psychological concept: learned helplessness. Developed by Martin Seligman in the 1960s through experiments with dogs, the concept describes how individuals who repeatedly experience uncontrollable negative events will stop trying to change their circumstances even when the opportunity later arises.

Vance applied this concept to understanding poverty in the Rust Belt. His argument was that many families in Middletown were paralyzed not only economically but psychologically — they no longer believed they could change their fate. Not because they were unintelligent or lazy, but because generations of failure had taught them that "effort doesn't work."

The Marine Corps cured his "learned helplessness." In his memoir, he wrote that four years of military service "cured what he called a 'crushing case of learned helplessness'" [Note 20]. The Corps taught him practical skills — how to manage a checkbook, how to handle finances, how to conduct himself as an adult — and, more importantly, it taught him a conviction: action can change outcomes.

1. Seligman's Original Model
Uncontrollable shocks → Dogs learn to stop trying to escape
Obstacles removed → Dogs still do not attempt
Conclusion: Repeated failure destroys the will to act
2. Vance's Community Application
Generational poverty → Community stops believing effort works
Cultural decay → Drug abuse, family breakdown
Conclusion: The Rust Belt's plight is a "mindset" problem requiring internal awakening
3. Critics' Counterargument
Deindustrialization → AK Steel layoffs, jobs vanished
Opioid crisis → Pharmaceutical companies deliberately pushed OxyContin
Conclusion: "Learned helplessness" is a symptom of structural collapse, not its cause

This psychological framework is the key to understanding Vance. It explains why he later attributed poverty primarily to "culture" rather than "institutions" — because in his personal experience, change really did come from an internal awakening, not an external intervention. Mamaw did not give him a government program; she gave him a conviction. The Marine Corps did not give him an economic stimulus plan; it gave him discipline.

But critics have pointed out that this framework has a fatal blind spot. It reduces systemic problems — deindustrialization, the opioid crisis, the lack of educational resources — to matters of personal mindset. Activists and scholars in the Appalachian region have pointed out that Vance ignored the following facts: when AK Steel laid off workers, "learned helplessness" was not the cause but the consequence; when pharmaceutical companies flooded small towns with OxyContin, "cultural decline" was not the root but the symptom.

This debate — whether poverty is a cultural problem or a structural one — would later become one of the deepest fault lines in American politics, and Vance planted himself firmly on the side of "culture."

Section VI

Ohio State to Yale — Two Worlds

After leaving the Marine Corps, Vance had accumulated enough credits from online university courses taken during his service. In 2009, he graduated from Ohio State University with dual bachelor's degrees in political science and philosophy [Note 21].

He applied to Yale Law School and was admitted — another fateful turning point. In New Haven, the appearance of three individuals would irrevocably alter his trajectory.

Amy Chua, the law professor famous for Battle Hymn of the Tiger Mother, became Vance's mentor. Chua encouraged him to write a memoir and ultimately served as the "godmother" of Hillbilly Elegy — Vance later called her his "authorial godmother" [Note 22]. Chua also set up Vance's romance with Usha Chilukuri, describing them as "an extremely unlikely pair, almost opposites in temperament" [Note 23].

Usha Chilukuri Vance, born in 1986 in San Diego County, California, is a Telugu Brahmin Indian American. Her parents immigrated to the United States from Andhra Pradesh in the 1980s — her father is a mechanical engineer who graduated from IIT Madras, and her mother is a molecular biology professor at the University of California, San Diego. She graduated from Yale College with highest honors (Summa cum laude), earned a master's degree at Clare College, Cambridge, and then entered Yale Law School [Note 24].

The two were married on June 14, 2014, in Kentucky in an interfaith ceremony — Vance's friend Jamil Jivani read from the Bible, and a Hindu priest blessed the couple. He was an atheist at the time (later converting to Catholicism in 2019), she was Hindu, and they decided to raise their children in the Christian faith [Note 25].

"Just a reminder, JD Vance is absolutely not under any influence from Peter Thiel. Thiel got him a job, funded his VC fund, donated $15 million to his PAC, helped him secure Trump's endorsement and the VP nomination... all because he believes in him!"

Peter Thiel, the Silicon Valley billionaire investor, was the third pivotal figure. Thiel gave a lecture at Yale Law School about technological stagnation, arguing that elite professionals were trapped climbing the socioeconomic ladder, sacrificing genuine happiness. The speech struck Vance with enormous force. Thiel introduced him to the ideas of the French philosopher Rene Girard — particularly the argument that Christianity transcends scapegoat mythology. Thiel also became Vance's guide into the world of venture capital, funding his firm Narya Capital and ultimately injecting $15 million into the super PAC that supported his Senate campaign [Note 26].

Twitter user @NickJFuentes summarized this relationship in his characteristic sarcastic tone. The tweet received over 15,000 likes [Note 27]. And @JasonBassler1 went further, listing the "five proofs" of Vance's dependence on Thiel [Note 28].

These criticisms were not without merit, but they overlooked a deeper paradox. Vance's experience at Yale was not merely one of being "co-opted" by Thiel — at Yale, he also completed a systematic reflection on his own life experiences. Together with Usha, he organized a discussion group on the topic of "social decline in white America" [Note 29]. This discussion group was the intellectual laboratory that would later produce Hillbilly Elegy.

In 2013, he graduated from Yale Law School, worked briefly at the law firm Sidley Austin and then at a San Francisco firm, before moving to Thiel's Mithril Capital as a principal, and ultimately founding his own venture capital firm, Narya Capital.

Section VII

A Delayed Explosion

"Even at my best, I was a delayed explosion — I could be defused, but it took skill and precision."

This sentence encapsulated the permanent brand that his Middletown childhood had left on him. The Marine Corps taught him discipline. Yale gave him knowledge. Usha gave him a stable family. But that "delayed bomb" was never fully defused. It was merely recalibrated — redirected from a Rust Belt youth who might have self-destructed in drugs and violence, into an ideological warrior who converted childhood trauma into political fuel.

His reaction to Obama is an illuminating case in point. In January 2017, as Obama was about to leave office, Vance wrote a surprisingly gracious passage in The New York Times: "At a pivotal time in my life, Barack Obama gave me hope that a boy who grew up like me could still achieve the most important of my dreams. For that, I'll miss him, and the example he set." [Note 31]

But in Hillbilly Elegy, he also acknowledged the Rust Belt's complex feelings toward Obama: "Barack Obama strikes at the heart of our deepest insecurities. He is a good father while many of us aren't. He wears suits to his job while we wear overalls... His wife tells us that we shouldn't be feeding our children certain foods, and we hate her for it — not because we think she's wrong, but because we know she's right." [Note 32]

The tension between these two passages — gratitude and resentment, identification and alienation — is the core tension of Vance's political personality. He was someone who emerged from the underclass: he understood its pain, yet could not forgive its self-destruction. He admired the Black president who had achieved the "American Dream," yet understood deeply that his own white working-class community's support for Trump was driven in part by this very sense of humiliation at being "lectured to by elites."

Epilogue

Seeds of Raw Material

When Vance left Yale and entered the venture capital world of Silicon Valley, he carried with him two suitcases: one filled with the memories of Middletown — the smell of drugs, Mamaw's stern love, the revolving door of father figures; the other filled with the analytical frameworks the Marine Corps and Yale had provided — "learned helplessness," personal responsibility, the chasm between elite narratives and ground-level reality.

In 2016, encouraged by Amy Chua, those suitcases were opened. Hillbilly Elegy: A Memoir of a Family and Culture in Crisis was published by HarperCollins on June 28, 2016, 264 pages, ultimately selling over 3 million copies [Note 33].

The book transformed him from an obscure venture capital investor into a national cultural symbol. It was embraced by conservatives as a "Bible" for understanding the white working class, and criticized by progressives as "repackaging welfare-state mythology as a white working-class primer" (in the words of Sarah Jones) [Note 34]. It became one of the most frequently cited "explanations" for the 2016 election.

But more importantly, the book revealed to Vance a possibility he had never imagined: the pain of Middletown was not merely personal trauma that needed to be healed — it was a resource that could be transformed, narrated, and politicized.

From son of the Rust Belt to Yale graduate, from Marine to Silicon Valley investor, from memoirist to Vice President of the United States — each step was built upon the rubble of the last. But those ruins were never fully cleared away.
