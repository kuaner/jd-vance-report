Chapter VI

The Iran TrapThe Iran Trap: When Anti-Warriors Get Strapped to the Chariot

On February 28, 2026, the military operation codenamed "Epic Fury" was launched. A vice president who had repeatedly promised during the campaign that "America will not go to war with Iran" found himself outside the decision room.

Section I

Diet Mountain Dew in the Situation Room

On February 28, 2026, the military operation codenamed Operation Epic Fury was formally launched. A joint U.S.-Israeli bomber fleet took off from multiple bases, unleashing ordnance upon Iran's ballistic missile facilities. The first strikes by B-2 stealth bombers marked the point of no return in this war.

At this historic moment, U.S. President Donald Trump commanded the situation room at Mar-a-Lago in Florida, flanked by Secretary of State Marco Rubio, Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth, and the National Security Advisor. Satellite imagery on the screens showed real-time bombing routes over the Persian Gulf.

And U.S. Vice President J.D. Vance — the man who had repeatedly promised during the campaign that "America will not go to war with Iran" — was not in that room.

White House official photo: Vance with Cabinet members in the Washington situation room, a can of Diet Mountain Dew at his side. Feb 28, 2026.

White House-released photos showed Vance left alone in the "upper floor" of the Washington situation room — connected to the chain of command via secure video conference. Beside him sat a can of Diet Mountain Dew and a grim-faced Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard. A report by La Voce di New York captured the absurdity of the scene in a single sentence: "When Washington was dropping bombs on Tehran, one face was missing from the small circle shaping the operation: JD Vance."

This image is stunning not because it exposes Vance's incompetence — but because it perfectly encapsulates his position within the entire administration. A political star who had once been eloquent in Senate hearings, who had turned European leaders pale at the Munich Security Conference, now sat in a corner of the situation room, clutching a diet soda, watching others fight a war he had opposed.

Vance's X account remained "unusually silent" throughout that entire weekend. No tweets, no statements, no personal remarks whatsoever on a war he should have helped decide. The silence itself was a form of rhetoric — it exposed his predicament more powerfully than any defense could.

On March 2, Marjorie Taylor Greene asked the question on the mind of the entire MAGA base during Megyn Kelly's show: "Where exactly is JD Vance?"

Section II

"The Humiliation of J.D. Vance"

On March 5, 2026, The Atlantic published a long-form essay by Idrees Kahloon titled "The Humiliation of J.D. Vance." This was no ordinary political commentary — it was the most precise surgical dissection to date of Vance's systematic marginalization within the White House.

The article's central thesis amounted to a single sentence: "Trump's war with Iran shows that, within the administration, the vice president's opinions matter less and less." [Note 1]

Kahloon's analysis unfolded along five threads of evidence, each pointing to the same conclusion: Vance is not an influential vice president — he is a display ornament.

Atlantic cover story illustration: "The Humiliation of J.D. Vance," Mar 5, 2026.

Thread One: The Total Collapse of Campaign Promises

Kahloon used Vance's own words against him. During the 2024 campaign, Vance told Tim Dillon: "Our interest, I think pretty obviously, is to not go to war with Iran. That would be a massive diversion of resources, it would be incredibly expensive for our country." On Shawn Ryan's podcast, he was even blunter: a war between Israel and Iran would be "the most likely, the most dangerous scenario to trigger World War III."

On February 26, 2026 — two days before the outbreak of war — Vance was still telling the Washington Post in an interview that he was a "skeptic of foreign military interventions" and that "we all favor diplomatic options."

Then the bombs fell. Vance's promises turned to ash in the smoke.

Thread Two: Total Defeat Across Five Policy Domains

The most devastating section of Kahloon's article was not about Iran — it was the panoramic power map it presented. On this map, Vance had been defeated in nearly every policy domain.

Foreign Policy: Vance was the isolationist wing of the MAGA coalition, armed with a coherent theoretical framework — America cannot fight on multiple fronts simultaneously, must not squander scarce munitions on regional conflicts because it still has to contend with a rising superpower in China. But the irony is that the very scenario he dreaded is unfolding: missile interceptors are being consumed at an alarming rate in a Middle Eastern discretionary war. On Ukraine, his ally Elbridge Colby managed to pause weapons shipments — but Trump reversed the decision within days, even expanding support for Ukraine. On Iran, he was bypassed entirely.

Economic Policy: Vance wanted to expand the child tax credit to $5,000 per child, protect American workers through expanded unionization, breaking up big tech companies, and accelerating antitrust enforcement. He championed "Hungarian-style" family policy. The reality? The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) only raised the child tax credit to $2,200. The Atlantic's assessment was incisive: "Aside from tariffs — which were Trump's decades-long obsession anyway — almost no Vanceism is visible." [Note 1]

AI Policy: At the 2025 Paris AI Summit, Vance delivered a major speech opposing "overregulation of AI" and pledging to "protect American AI and chip technology from theft and misuse." Yet by December 2025, the Trump administration authorized the export of advanced chips to China in exchange for a 25% revenue share. In March 2026, the administration attempted to suppress Anthropic. What Vance said in Paris, nobody in Washington remembered.

Immigration Policy: Although Vance's anti-immigration stance during the campaign aligned with the MAGA base, The Atlantic pointed out that the real power broker in this domain is Stephen Miller — his influence far exceeds Vance's.

Culture War: Some of Vance's signature themes are reflected in the administration, but The Atlantic's judgment was clear: Miller clearly wields greater authority.

Thread Three: "Swift Acquiescence" in the Leaked Signal Chats

Kahloon obtained an inadvertently leaked Signal chat log. In it, Vance had expressed opposition to strikes against Houthi forces in private communications, calling them "wrong." But after this dissent was overruled — The Atlantic used a precise word — he "acquiesced" (swiftly fell in line). [Note 1]

This is not normal behavior for a politician. The normal pattern is: you oppose internally, and if overruled, you maintain public silence or express reservations in some fashion. But Vance's pattern is: oppose internally, defend fully externally.

This "internal opposition — full external defense" pattern appeared not only in the Houthi issue but throughout the entire Iran war process. A March 13 POLITICO report cited two anonymous senior White House officials, one of whom described Vance's role: "His role is to provide the president and the administration with... all the perspectives on what might happen from many different angles... but once a decision is made, he is fully supportive." [Note 2] The other was more direct: "Vance was skeptical of American strikes on Iran before Trump decided to launch the war." [Note 2]

The speed of the switch between "skeptical" and "fully supportive" is dizzying.

Thread Four: Allies Forced Out

The Atlantic article revealed two personnel conflicts that more clearly than any policy disagreement demonstrated the erosion of Vance's power.

Gail Slater, Vance's former policy advisor, was forced out by Attorney General Pam Bondi from her position leading antitrust enforcement at the Department of Justice. Her deputy Roger Alford was even more blunt when he was ousted last year, accusing "MAGA-in-Name-Only lobbyists" of seeking to "enrich themselves." [Note 1]

These were not Vance's casual acquaintances. Slater was a core antitrust policy figure he had worked with since his Senate days. Alford's accusations pointed directly at the corruption of the MAGA movement — the very movement Vance claimed he wanted to reform.

Thread Five: The Fall of Elbridge Colby

Elbridge Colby, the Pentagon's highest-ranking policy official, was Vance's core ally on defense policy. In the summer of 2025, Colby successfully made a decision Vance would certainly have endorsed: pausing weapons shipments to Ukraine. But Trump reversed the decision within days.

The Atlantic used this story to illustrate the rout of the Vance faction across the entire administration — it was not merely that his personal influence was waning, but that the ideological faction he represented was being systematically purged.

Kahloon's Conclusion: The Identity Cage

The concluding paragraph of the Atlantic article is worth quoting in full:

"Vance came to public attention as a resounding critic of Trump who could nonetheless explain his appeal among heartland Americans to coastal elites. [...] Reinvention is obviously possible for a man who has reinvented himself before. But if he seeks the Republican nomination for president in 2028, he may find himself bound to an unpopular series of policies that the 2024 version of Vance would oppose."

Idrees Kahloon, The Atlantic, March 5, 2026 [Note 1]

This returns us to the core concept discussed in Chapter III: the plasticity of identity. Each of Vance's transformations had been celebrated as evidence of flexibility, but inside the White House, that plasticity became a cage — he had constructed so many layers of identity that when the moment came to make a genuine choice, he no longer knew which layer was real.

Section III

Vance vs Rubio: Two Versions of Loyalty

If the Atlantic article performed an internal dissection of Vance's power decline, Ed Kilgore's analysis in New York Magazine provided the external perspective: through the contrast between Vance and Marco Rubio, it revealed two fundamentally different political trajectories. [Note 3]

Kilgore cited an observation by Jonathan V. Last that is key to understanding Vance's power base:

"His base of power isn't Republican voters, but Republican elites. He's spent his entire adult life currying favor with more powerful patrons — progressing from Amy Chua, to David Frum, to Peter Thiel, to Tucker Carlson, and finally to Donald Trump's children."

Jonathan V. Last, via NY Magazine [Note 3]

"Instead of making himself popular with voters, Vance chose to work the inside game. He targeted people with enough juice to give him the prizes he wanted."

Jonathan V. Last, via NY Magazine [Note 3]

This passage explains Vance's behavioral pattern during the Iran war. He is not a vice president who uses public opinion to influence decisions — he is a vice president who uses patron-client relationships to acquire power. When the interests of his patrons conflict, he has no public opinion to fall back on.

Rubio's path has been entirely different. From being insulted by Trump as "Lil' Marco" in 2016 to becoming Secretary of State and National Security Advisor in 2026, Rubio has always operated within the Republican establishment. He possesses something Vance lacks: an independent voter base (Florida's Cuban-American community, conservative evangelicals, defense hawks) and decades of accumulated relationships on Capitol Hill.

The contrast between these two men reached its extreme at three critical moments.

The Venezuela Operation: Hawks vs. Post Hoc Rationalizers

In the Venezuela operation in early 2026, Rubio was the driving force. As Secretary of State, he played a central role throughout the planning and execution — from intelligence gathering to diplomatic coordination to military deployment. What was Vance's role? He was the post-facto "defender." He framed the military operation as a "law enforcement action" — a narrative that sounded less like war.

Hawks design the war; doves defend it — this role assignment is itself a humiliation. It is not that Vance chose this role; it is that this was the only role he was permitted to play.

The Iran War: The Absent Dove vs. The Hawk in the Spotlight

In the Iran war, the contrast became even sharper. Rubio was in the Mar-a-Lago situation room, standing beside Trump, monitoring every wave of bombings. Vance was in Washington's upper floor, clutching a Diet Mountain Dew, listening in via video conference.

After the outbreak of war, Rubio gained enormous media exposure as Secretary of State worldwide — UN speeches, congressional hearings, television interviews. What about Vance? He was assigned to lead the "War on Fraud" — a role that sounds like an administrative task rather than a national strategy.

"No need for Vance to sell the war to the MAGA base — they're already gung-ho because of Trump's statements. No need for the VP to cheerlead — all of Trump's media outlets are already pushing the war at full throttle."

Ed Kilgore, NY Magazine / Intelligencer [Note 3]

Vance's primary role was defined as "protecting Trump's MAGA flank on social media." But even this role became redundant — because Trump himself is the greatest protector of the MAGA flank.

The Munich Legacy

In Chapter V we discussed the Munich Security Conference — Vance's 2025 speech sent shockwaves through Europe; a year later, Rubio took his place at the conference while Vance's portrait was artfully obscured by plants in the venue. This was no coincidence — it was a symbolic transfer of power. Vance was the speaker; Rubio is the executor.

Trump came to power in 2016 as an anti-war candidate and won reelection in 2024 on anti-war promises. The campaign slogan — "No more wars, end these stupid, pointless wars" — had been one of the MAGA movement's most fundamental commitments. When the bombs fell, the sound of that promise shattering reverberated within the MAGA camp.

Defection of Key Figures

Joe Rogan said on his March 10 podcast: "People feel betrayed," noting the campaign promise was "No more wars, end these stupid, pointless wars." Rogan is no ordinary figure — he was one of Vance's most crucial media allies during the 2024 election, and his podcast was Vance's primary channel for reaching independent and young voters. When Rogan uses the word "betrayed," it inflicts more damage on Vance than any mainstream media criticism. [Note 4]

Tucker Carlson, one of Vance's closest ideological allies, called the war "evil" in an ABC News interview. David Sacks, Vance's close ally and White House AI czar, said America should "declare victory and leave" Iran. [Note 4]

These voices formed an unsettling chorus: virtually all of Vance's most important ideological allies stood on the opposite side of the war.

And those who stood on the side of war?

Laura Loomer, a right-wing influencer, called on social media for Vance to condemn Tucker Carlson, warning that otherwise "Marco will be the nominee." This threat struck at Vance's political jugular: if you don't stand with Trump's war, donors will go to Rubio. [Note 4]

"I Don't Want to Go to Prison"

The most-engaged Iran war-related tweet on Twitter came from @allenAnalysis (44,035 likes), featuring a video of Vance responding to a question about "what advice he gave Trump before the war": "I don't want to go to prison." [Note 7]

This line became viral material not because it was particularly outrageous — but because it distilled Vance's predicament in the most concise language possible. An anti-war advocate asked why he supports the war, and his answer was not about national interest, not about geopolitical strategy, not about democracy versus tyranny — it was about himself. About the personal consequences he might face. It reduced a grand foreign policy question to a matter of personal survival.

Jackson Hinkle's tweet (7,990 likes) deployed an even simpler frame: "Iraq war 2.0." [Note 8] The power of this analogy lies in its immediacy — no explanation needed. Anyone with a memory of the 2003 Iraq War would instantly grasp its meaning: the intelligence could be fabricated, the war could be wrong, the consequences could be catastrophic.

Grassroots Anger on Reddit

The Reddit data is even more alarming. A post about Vance on r/MurderedByWords scored 49,464 points (the highest on that subreddit), with the title "He learned it from his boss too!" — referring to Vance's hypocrisy: once anti-war, now pro-war, mirroring Trump's behavioral pattern. [Note 6]

A post on r/NewsSource about "Vance's town hall indefinitely postponed due to unpopular Iran war" scored 3,324 points and 76 comments. [Note 6] This news was barely noticed in mainstream media — but it revealed the concrete impact of the war on Vance's day-to-day political activities. He could barely hold public events.

A post on r/BoomersBeingFools (1,457 points) placed Vance's quotes side by side: the campaign promise "We don't want to go to war with Iran" versus the war defense "But we want peace, provided they don't have a nuclear program." [Note 6] This "before-and-after" technique is extraordinarily effective on social media because it makes hypocrisy visual.

A Twitter Anomaly: The Volume of Opposition

Cross-topic data comparison revealed an important pattern: on the Munich speech topic, the Twitter support-to-opposition ratio was 12:3; on 2028 prospects, it was 9:2; but on the Iran war topic, it was 1:8. [Note 6] This means that even within the MAGA-dominated Twitter ecosystem, the opposition triggered by the Iran war was overwhelming.

Even the tweets that "supported" Vance were mostly "defensive" support — not proactive praise for his position, but pushback against journalists' questions. This suggests a deeper problem: Vance's supporters could not find a reason worth celebrating in this war; all they could do was try to block criticism.

Section VI

Trump's Public Humiliation

On March 9, Trump did something extraordinarily rare in presidential history: he publicly acknowledged that his own vice president disagreed with him.

Trump said Vance was "philosophically a little bit different" on the war, "less enthusiastic." [Note 9]

On the surface, this language was mild — "philosophically different" sounds like a respectful acknowledgment of dissent. But its practical effect was devastating. When the U.S. president publicly states that his vice president is "philosophically different" from him on an ongoing war, he is effectively telling the entire world: this vice president is not in the decision-making circle.

Historically, presidents publicly acknowledging disagreements with their vice presidents is exceedingly rare. Such differences are normally resolved behind closed doors — because exposing them publicly weakens the administration's unity and undermines the vice president's credibility in international diplomacy. Trump chose to expose this disagreement publicly — either he truly does not care about Vance's face, or he is deliberately sending a signal.

Vance's response revealed a deeper predicament. On March 13, he told the media: "I think it's important that the president of the United States can have conversations with his team, and his team isn't running to the American media to, you know, say all kinds of things." [Note 2] This sounded like criticism of leaking officials, but its subtext was more telling — he was asking for something he did not possess: to be taken seriously.

On March 16, Vance publicly endorsed Trump's Iran war decision, accusing the media of trying to drive a wedge between them. On March 18, he made a rare direct comment in Michigan, asserting that Trump would not get dragged into a protracted conflict. [Note 10] From "unusually silent" to "openly defending" — the transformation took less than two weeks. But the speed of the transformation is itself the problem — it once again confirmed the Atlantic's characterization of "acquiesced."

One month later, this "most powerful vice president" was excluded from the war decision circle, sitting in the situation room's upper floor, drinking Diet Mountain Dew.

This precipitous power decline was not accidental — it had a clear structural architecture.

Section VIII

The Unsolvable Trap

Vance's predicament can be reduced to a binary choice: support Trump's war decision → lose the anti-war MAGA base; oppose → offend Trump and lose the ticket to the 2028 nomination.

But this is not a genuine binary choice — because both options lead to the same destination.

If he chooses to support the war — as he already has — he will lose those in the MAGA camp who truly believe in "America First" and anti-interventionism. These people will not defect to the Democrats, but they may stay home in the 2028 primaries, or turn to Rubio (whose hawkish stance on the war is, ironically, more consistent).

If he chooses to oppose the war — as he did internally — he will offend Trump, who is the sole arbiter of the Republican nomination process. Without Trump's endorsement, Vance has almost no chance in the 2028 primaries.

This is the ultimate expression of "learned helplessness" at the political level. Vance once believed he could influence decisions through ideas — his anti-interventionist theory, his "post-liberal" conservatism, his "America First" foreign policy framework. But when the real test came, he discovered he had no seat at the decision table.

This is the continuation of the same thread of contradiction seen in the Munich speech. In Munich, Vance said "the threat comes from within" — America should not intervene in external affairs. The premise of that statement was his belief that he had the capacity within the administration to shape policy direction. But everything that happened after Munich — the convergence of the Paris AI Summit and the Silicon Valley agenda, Rubio taking the Munich baton, Vance's portrait obscured by plants — had already signaled that his influence was waning. The Iran war merely confirmed the trend.

"Any Trump vice president is always one step away from being discarded." Mike Pence was loyal to the end — until he was asked to break the law, and then he was marginalized. Vance is loyal — until he is asked to defend a war he opposed — and then what?

Ed Kilgore, NY Magazine [Note 3]

White House spokesperson Anna Kelly's denial only made things worse: "Efforts to create a rift between President Trump and Vice President Vance are completely misleading. The president listens to a variety of viewpoints from his brilliant national security team..." [Note 2] If the president truly "listens to a variety of viewpoints," why was Vance excluded from the Mar-a-Lago core decision circle? If Vance is the president's "tremendous asset," why was he in the situation room's "upper floor" instead of at the main table?

Vance spokesperson Taylor Van Kirk's response further exposed the problem: "The vice president has been the constant target of leaks from both sides, with people trying to project their own views onto him... The vice president is a proud member of the president's national security team, and he keeps his counsel to the president private." [Note 2] "Keeps his counsel private" — but the Atlantic's leaked Signal chats had already demonstrated that Vance's "private counsel" was met with "swift acquiescence" after being overruled. The value of private counsel lies not in its existence, but in whether it influenced the decision.

Section IX

Fundraising: The Only Thing Left to Do

When Vance could not influence the war, what could he do?

The answer was fundraising. On March 12, 2026 — the very same day NY Magazine published its scathing analysis — Axios reported that Vance would attend a fundraiser at Joe Lonsdale's Austin residence. Admission: $50,000 per person, $100,000 per couple. Attendees included major donors from the finance and cryptocurrency sectors such as JPMorgan Chase, Gemini, and Crypto.com. [Note 12]

Section X

The Road to 2028

The warning at the end of the Atlantic article is becoming increasingly concrete: if Vance seeks the 2028 Republican nomination, "he may find himself bound to an unpopular series of policies that the 2024 version of Vance would oppose." [Note 1]

The Iran war has made this question no longer hypothetical.

If the war ends in the short term, Vance can scrape by — he can argue that his "skepticism" was vindicated (the war was indeed unnecessary) while his "loyalty" was also validated (he publicly supported the president). But this narrative requires two conditions to hold simultaneously: the war must end quickly, and Trump must allow him to frame it this way.

If the war becomes a quagmire — if U.S. casualties continue to climb, if gas prices remain high, if Iranian resistance proves more tenacious than expected — then Vance's position will deteriorate sharply. He will be condemned from both sides: by his former base as a "traitor" (you promised no war), and by the establishment as "disloyal" (you opposed the war internally). Curt Mills' judgment — "For core MAGA who believe the promise to remake the party and the movement, this is just a tremendous letdown" — will become a political force. [Note 5]

And looming over all of this, Marco Rubio is rising as a hawk. Prediction markets already show Rubio overtaking Vance in general election odds. A "Draft Rubio" movement has emerged among donors. [Note 3] Trump himself has been asking his advisors, both publicly and privately, whether he prefers Vance or Rubio.

From "Lil' Marco" to the potential 2028 Republican nominee — Rubio has walked a path that Vance never did. He accumulated power within the establishment, built an independent voter base, and stood beside the president during wartime rather than in the situation room's upper floor.

Vance's road to 2028 now passes through a battlefield he personally promised would never exist.
