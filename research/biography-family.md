## 2. Early Life and Family Background

### 2.1 Birth and Name Changes

JD Vance was born **James Donald Bowman** on August 2, 1984, in Middletown, Ohio -- a small Rust Belt city in Butler County, southwestern Ohio. His parents, Don and Bev Bowman, came from Scots-Irish ancestry with deep Appalachian roots in Breathitt County and Jackson, Kentucky.

His name has changed multiple times over his life:

1. **James Donald Bowman** (1984-1990) -- Birth name, named after biological father Donald Bowman
2. **James David Hamel** (1990-2013) -- When mother Beverly married her third husband Bob Hamel, who adopted young JD at age 6. Beverly changed the middle name from "Donald" to "David" (her uncle's name) to preserve the "J.D." nickname while erasing the biological father's name. As Vance later wrote: "This seemed a bit of a stretch even when I was six. Any old D name would have done, so long as it wasn't Donald."
3. **James David Vance** (2013-present) -- After his mother and adoptive father Bob Hamel divorced, and his maternal grandparents became his primary caretakers, he took his maternal grandmother Bonnie Vance's maiden name. He told the *New York Times* it was to honor his grandmother "Mamaw."

Vance legally shortened "James David" to "J.D." during his Senate run.

![JD Vance campaign in Racine, Wisconsin, 2024](assets/jd-vance-campaign-racine.jpg)

### 2.2 Parents

**Mother: Beverly "Bev" Vance (formerly Bowman, Hamel, Aikins)**

Beverly had JD's older half-sister Lindsay in 1979 at age 19, then JD in 1984. She worked as a nurse, which gave her access to prescription medications that she began to abuse. She was married five times, creating what Vance described as a "revolving door of father figures." She struggled with substance abuse for over 15 years, causing extreme dysfunction and sometimes physical aggression at home.

Key incidents described in *Hillbilly Elegy*:
- When JD was 12, Beverly was arrested after a car incident where she allegedly sped up and threatened to crash the car and kill them both. JD fled to a neighbor's house who called the police. He later told Megyn Kelly: "In that moment I just felt relieved and I thought to myself, 'Alright I'm going to live another day.'"
- She was fired from her nursing job for erratic behavior and stealing pills.
- She overdosed on heroin during JD's time at Yale Law School.

After years of addiction, Bev achieved sobriety. She has been sober for over 10 years. In 2024, at the Republican National Convention, Vance publicly celebrated her recovery. In September 2025, Vice President Vance hosted her at the White House Roosevelt Room to mark her sobriety milestone. She now speaks publicly about addiction recovery under the title "A Story of Addiction, Hope, Recovery and Redemption."

**Father: Donald Bowman**

Donald walked out on the family when JD was a toddler. His parents officially divorced when JD was 6. Beverly told JD he would "never see his biological father again." Vance wrote: "Of all the things I hated about my childhood, nothing compared to the revolving door of father figures." Donald briefly re-entered JD's life during his teenage years, criticizing his music tastes (Black Sabbath, Led Zeppelin) and advising him to listen to Christian rock instead.

### 2.3 Maternal Grandparents -- "Mamaw" and "Papaw"

**Bonnie Blanton Vance ("Mamaw")** and **James Vance ("Papaw")** are the central figures of Vance's upbringing. They relocated from Jackson, Kentucky (Breathitt County) to Middletown, Ohio after World War II as part of the Great Migration of Appalachian families seeking industrial work.

Vance calls them "without question or qualification, the best things that ever happened to me." After Beverly's behavior became untenable, JD was placed in their care. Mamaw was stern but loving -- she discharged herself from the hospital (where she was being treated for pneumonia) to take JD in after he crashed her car during a period of acting out with friends. She told him: "Someone will need to care for the family when she is gone, and that he has the choice to make something of himself."

Papaw died in 2005 while JD was in the Marines. Mamaw died in 2005 as well. In his 2022 Senate victory speech, Vance said: "You're not always going to agree with every vote that I take...but I will never forget the woman who raised me."

### 2.4 Sibling

**Lindsay Ratliff** (nee Bowman) -- JD's older half-sister, born in 1979. She is approximately 5 years older than JD. She was raised alongside him in Middletown and now has her own family. She played a significant role in caring for their mother during her overdoses and recovery.

### 2.5 Middletown, Ohio

Middletown (population ~51,000) is a Rust Belt city between Dayton and Cincinnati, once anchored by AK Steel (formerly Armco). Vance described it as a town of primarily low-income residents where "poverty was a family tradition" for many families with Appalachian roots.

Many Middletown residents were upset at the portrayal in *Hillbilly Elegy*. Vance later told his cousin: "I don't blame Middletown for the dysfunction that existed in our family...Some of the Middletown defensiveness is rooted in pride of place. I'm proud of Middletown." But he added: "because of forces outside of Middletown there are families who grow up below the poverty line. There is a lot of drug abuse."

The city has since installed signs welcoming visitors to the "hometown of JD Vance."

## 3. Military Career

### 3.1 Enlistment

In April 2003, a month after the U.S. invasion of Iraq, 18-year-old Vance (then James David Hamel) walked into a military recruiter's office in Middletown, accompanied by his older cousin Rachael, a former Marine. He had originally planned to attend Ohio State University but had second thoughts about his mediocre grades and confusing financial aid paperwork.

"There was college, or nothing, or the Marines," he wrote in *Hillbilly Elegy*, "and I didn't like either of the first two options."

Most of his family was vehemently opposed. He reported to **Parris Island, South Carolina** for 13 weeks of boot camp, followed by Combat Training School in North Carolina and public affairs training at the Defense Information School at Fort Meade, Maryland.

### 3.2 Service at Cherry Point

Vance scored high enough on his aptitude test to become a public affairs Marine. In July 2004, he was assigned to the **2nd Marine Aircraft Wing** at **Marine Corps Air Station Cherry Point** in eastern North Carolina, achieving the rank of Corporal (E-4).

At Cherry Point, he worked in a narrow building on the airfield writing articles for the base's official newspaper, *The Windsock*. Fellow Marines remember him:
- Devouring copies of *The Economist* and engaging in lengthy policy debates
- "Nerding out over John McCain more than a normal person would"
- Reading novels by libertarian writer Ayn Rand
- Having a knack for solving a Rubik's Cube quickly
- Being told by a fellow Marine who superimposed his face over George W. Bush: "You could just tell by looking at him that he always had a plan"

### 3.3 Iraq Deployment

In August 2005, Vance deployed to **Al-Asad Airbase** in western Iraq for six months as a **combat correspondent** with a public affairs team. His role:
- Embedded with soldiers, documenting their missions in short articles for military publications and local U.S. papers
- When "outside the wire," carried a notebook, camera, and rifle
- Wrote routine dispatches -- covering military concerts, cake-cutting ceremonies, and base-building efforts
- Occasionally traveled to forward operating bases and polling stations

The base was relatively safe by Iraqi standards (nicknamed "Camp Cupcake" for its Burger King and Pizza Hut), but incoming mortar fire was common. On arrival day, Vance was greeted by a shelter-in-place warning.

**Key experience:** During the Iraqi constitutional referendum and parliamentary elections (late 2005), Vance was assigned to provide security at polling stations. He was reading Natan Sharansky's *The Case for Democracy* at the time and was struck by the gap between the book's ideals and the reality on the ground. The Iraqis he met "didn't really care about democracy that much...they just want a safe country."

He also witnessed Dick Cheney's visit to Al-Asad in December 2005. When a Marine corporal pressed Cheney about the apparent stalemate, Cheney replied: "Well, Iraq's looking good." In private, Vance had begun to seriously doubt these assurances.

Vance **did not see combat** during his deployment, a fact he has acknowledged.

### 3.4 Return and Discharge

Vance's deployment ended in March 2006. Back at Cherry Point, his supervisor Shawn Haney selected him for special assignments -- first as community-relations liaison, then as media officer (normally a captain's role). During a May 2007 airshow, a civilian plane crashed near the base, and Vance became the point person for media response.

He was discharged from the Marine Corps in **September 2007** and returned to Ohio to start as a freshman at Ohio State University.

![JD Vance's Iraq story -- Politico Magazine cover](assets/vance-iraq-politico.jpg)

### 3.5 Impact of Military Service

Vance is the **first veteran of the post-9/11 wars** to serve as vice president, and the first veteran of any sort since Al Gore. His four years in the Marines:
- Cured what he called a "crushing case of learned helplessness"
- Taught him practical skills: balancing a checkbook, managing finances, conducting himself as an adult
- Planted the seeds of his political skepticism and anti-interventionist foreign policy
- Gave him material for his later writing on the disconnect between elite rhetoric and working-class experience
- Gave him a close circle of friends, several of whom attended his 2014 wedding

## 4. Education

### 4.1 Ohio State University (2007-2009)

After leaving the Marines, Vance enrolled at Ohio State University. He took online classes for college credit even before his discharge. He graduated with a **Bachelor of Arts in Political Science and Philosophy** (double major) in **2009**.

### 4.2 Yale Law School (2010-2013)

Vance was admitted to Yale Law School and earned his **Juris Doctor in 2013**. At Yale, he:

- Met his future wife Usha Chilukuri (encouraged by their professor Amy Chua)
- Collaborated with Usha to organize a discussion group focused on "social decline in white America"
- Was encouraged by Professor **Amy Chua** (author of *Battle Hymn of the Tiger Mother*) to write his memoir -- Chua became the "authorial godmother" of *Hillbilly Elegy*
- Was influenced by venture capitalist **Peter Thiel**, who spoke at Yale and argued that elite professionals trapped themselves climbing socioeconomic ladders at the expense of happiness. Thiel introduced Vance to the thought of French philosopher **Rene Girard**
- Worked summers at Sidley Austin LLP and other firms
- Graduated and began working at a San Francisco law firm before moving into venture capital

## 7. Hillbilly Elegy -- Cultural Impact

### 7.1 The Book

**Hillbilly Elegy: A Memoir of a Family and Culture in Crisis**

- **Published:** June 28, 2016 by HarperCollins
- **Pages:** 264
- **Subject:** Rural sociology, poverty, family drama
- **Sales:** Over 3 million copies; 2016 bestseller
- **Authorial origin:** Encouraged by Yale professor Amy Chua, who persuaded Vance to write it
- **Sequel:** $8 million deal signed in 2017; as of 2026, not yet published

The book describes Vance's upbringing in Middletown, Ohio, and summers in Jackson, Kentucky. It argues that hillbilly culture fosters social disintegration and economic insecurity, and attributes Appalachia's political swing from Democratic to Republican to these dynamics. Vance describes "learned helplessness" and his grandmother as his savior.

### 7.2 Critical Reception

**Positive:**
- *New York Times* (Jennifer Senior): "A compassionate, discerning sociological analysis of the white underclass"
- Brookings Institution report noted it "anecdotally confirmed" the conclusion that family stability is essential to upward mobility
- Praised by conservative commentators including Mona Charen, Reihan Salam, Rod Dreher, and John Podhoretz
- Won 2017 Audie Award for Nonfiction
- German Chancellor Olaf Scholz said the book "moved me to tears"
- An interview in *The American Conservative* by Rod Dreher was so popular it crashed the magazine's website

**Negative:**
- Critics said Vance generalized too much from personal experience in suburban Ohio
- Sarah Jones (*The New Republic*): "the false prophet of Blue America," "a list of myths about welfare queens repackaged as a primer on the white working class"
- Jared Yates Sexton (*Salon*): criticized "damaging rhetoric" and for endorsing policies that "gut the poor"
- Bob Hutton (*Jacobin*): "relies on circular logic and eugenics," "primarily a work of self-congratulation"
- Sarah Smarsh (*The Guardian*): "most downtrodden whites are not conservative male Protestants from Appalachia"
- **Appalachian Reckoning: A Region Responds to Hillbilly Elegy** -- anthology of essays criticizing Vance's generalizations
- Middletown residents were upset at being portrayed as a "hillbilly town"

### 7.3 Political Weaponization

The book's 2016 publication coincided with the rise of Donald Trump. It was widely cited as an "explanation" for why white working-class voters supported Trump. Vance himself initially criticized Trump during the 2016 election cycle ("I can't stomach Trump," he told NPR, saying Trump was "leading the white working class to a very dark place"). However:

- Vance walked back criticism when entering the 2022 Senate race
- Endorsed Trump and aligned with MAGA movement
- After being named Trump's VP pick in July 2024, book sales surged by over 600,000 copies in a week
- 150,000 copies sold in 24 hours after the VP announcement
- Netflix viewership of the film jumped 1,179%

## 8. Religious Journey

### 8.1 Childhood: Minimal Church Attendance

Vance was not baptized as a child. His grandmother "Mamaw" believed in Jesus and liked Billy Graham but disliked "organized religion." Family members generally didn't attend church except when visiting relatives in Jackson, Kentucky.

At age ~10, Vance asked Mamaw: "Does God love us?" Her reaction caused her to cry.

### 8.2 Teenage Years: Pentecostal Phase

As a teenager, Vance reconnected with his biological father, who had become a serious Pentecostal. Vance attended a large Pentecostal church in southeastern Ohio:

- Embraced millennialism, including belief the world would end in 2007
- Rejected evolution
- Became a "devoted convert"
- Later wrote: "I'm not sure if I liked the structure or if I just wanted to share in something that was important to him"

### 8.3 Young Adult: Atheism

During his Marine Corps years, Vance drifted into atheism:

- Was influenced by "New Atheist" writers: **Richard Dawkins, Sam Harris, Christopher Hitchens**
- particularly taken by Hitchens, who defended the Iraq War
- Encouraged fellow Marines to read Ayn Rand novels
- Remained atheist through much of his time at Yale

### 8.4 The Path to Catholicism

Several influences drew Vance toward Catholicism:

1. **Basil Mitchell** (Anglican philosopher) -- Encountered at Ohio State. Mitchell's concept of faith as a "trial" requiring trust without full understanding cracked Vance's atheism. "Here was Mitchell, conceding that the brokenness of the world and our individual tribulations did, in fact, count against the existence of God. But not definitively."

2. **Peter Thiel** -- Introduced Vance to **Rene Girard**, whose writings on Christianity as transcending the scapegoat myth resonated. Christ "has not wronged the civilization; the civilization has wronged him."

3. **Usha Vance** -- Supported his decision to convert despite being Hindu herself. Helped him work through anger from his chaotic childhood.

4. **Dominican friars** -- "A few informal conversations" led to serious study. Vance described "some weird coincidences" including a wine glass seemingly leaping off a bar shelf during a theological debate, and hearing the same Psalm chanted by friars that he'd been listening to on a train.

### 8.5 Baptism and Conversion

- **Baptized Catholic in August 2019** at age 35 by Dominican Father Henry Stephan at St. Gertrude Priory, attached to a Dominican parish in Cincinnati
- Confirmation saint: **St. Augustine**
- Called his conversion "joining the resistance"
- Credited practicing Catholicism with making him more patient, better at controlling his temper, more forgiving, and more family-oriented

Vance wrote a **6,777-word conversion essay** for the Easter 2020 issue of *The Lamp*, a Catholic magazine.

### 8.6 Public Religious Statements

Vance has said: **"I believe the devil is real and that he works terrible things in our society"** -- a statement some called "a crazy conspiracy theory."

In 2025, during a Turning Point USA event, Vance was asked about his Hindu wife potentially going to hell, and implied that non-Christians face that fate -- comments that sparked significant controversy. He later said he wants Usha to "somehow be moved" by Christianity, framing it as "an act of compassion."
